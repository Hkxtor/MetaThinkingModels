<define>
A thought experiment is a mental exercise that explores hypothetical scenarios to test ideas, reveal paradoxes, or solve problems without physical experimentation. By stripping away real-world constraints, it allows us to isolate variables, challenge assumptions, and refine theories.
</define>

<example>
Physics (Einstein's Relativity)
Experiment: "What if you chased a light beam at its own speed?" (Einstein, age 16).
Insight: This led to the realization that light's speed is constant, a cornerstone of special relativity.
Problem Solved: Reconciling Newtonian mechanics with electromagnetism.
</example>

<example>
Ethics (The Trolley Problem)
Scenario: "Should you pull a lever to divert a runaway trolley, killing one person to save five?"
Insight: Exposes tensions between utilitarianism ("greater good") and individual rights.
Application: Used to debate AI ethics, self-driving car algorithms, and legal principles.
</example>

<example>
Computer Science (The Chinese Room Argument)
Scenario: "Can a person following instructions in a book (without understanding Chinese) simulate intelligence?" (John Searle).
Insight: Challenges whether AI can ever truly "understand" or just mimic behavior.
Impact: Shapes debates about consciousness in machine learning.
</example>
