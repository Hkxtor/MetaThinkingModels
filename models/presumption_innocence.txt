<define>
A legal principle stating that a person is considered innocent until proven guilty in a court of law. It places the burden of proof on the accuser (e.g., prosecutors) and ensures fair treatment by:
Prohibiting premature punishment.
Requiring evidence beyond reasonable doubt.
Protecting against biased assumptions.
</define>

<example>
Criminal Justice (Legal Trials)
Example: A defendant accused of theft cannot be jailed before trial unless evidence justifies detention.
Violation: Media labeling suspects as "criminals" before verdicts.
Impact: Ensures wrongful arrests don't ruin lives prematurely.
</example>

<example>
Academia (Plagiarism Cases)
Example: A student flagged for plagiarism gets a hearing to present their side before penalties are applied.
Violation: Professors failing grades based solely on algorithmic plagiarism detectors.
Impact: Protects students from erroneous academic penalties.
</example>

<example>
Social Media (Online "Canceling")
Example: A celebrity accused of misconduct isn't banned from platforms until verified evidence emerges.
Violation: Immediate deplatforming based on viral allegations (e.g., 2020 "cancel culture" trends).
Impact: Reduces mob justice and reputational harm.
</example>
